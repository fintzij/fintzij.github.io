Jon Fintzi

Associate Director, Statistical Methodology & Innovation
Bristol Myers Squibb

I'm a biostatistician working at the intersection of methodological research and clinical drug development. I develop and apply novel statistical methods — Bayesian adaptive designs, multistate models, disease progression models, and simulation-based approaches — to support decision-making across therapeutic areas including neuroscience, oncology, infectious disease, and immunology.

Previously, I spent six years at the NIAID Biostatistics Research Branch, where I served as lead statistician on clinical trials and cross-institutional collaborations for COVID-19, malaria, Ebola, and allergic diseases. I hold a Ph.D. in Biostatistics from the University of Washington.

Research Interests

Adaptive & Bayesian Trial Design

Innovative clinical trial designs that incorporate accumulating data, external information, and Bayesian decision frameworks to improve efficiency and ethical balance.

Multistate & Survival Models

Semi-Markov and multistate models for disease progression, clinical outcomes, and interval-censored data, with applications in neurodegenerative disease and infectious disease.

Estimands & Causal Inference

Frameworks for defining treatment effects in the presence of intercurrent events, including work on SMART designs and the ICH E9(R1) estimand framework.

Statistical Computation

High-performance computing, MCMC methods, simulation-based trial design optimization, and open-source software development in R, Julia, and C++.

Epidemic Modeling

Stochastic compartmental models for outbreak surveillance, transmission dynamics, and vaccine efficacy estimation under partially observed data.

Benefit-Risk & Decision Analysis

Simulation strategies for quantifying probability of technical success, benefit-risk trade-offs, and supporting portfolio-level decision-making in drug development.

Open-Source Software

MultistateModels.jl

Julia

Simulation and inference for multistate models, especially semi-Markov models, fit to panel data. Supports flexible hazard specifications, exact and approximate likelihoods, and Bayesian inference.

★ 6

stemr

R

Approximate Bayesian inference for stochastic epidemic models via the linear noise approximation. Designed for fitting compartmental models to partially observed incidence and prevalence data.

★ 9

BDAepimodel

R

Tractable fitting of stochastic epidemic models via Bayesian data augmentation. Implements MCMC algorithms for SIR-type models with exact likelihood computation.

★ 5

ECctmc

C++

Simulation of sample paths for endpoint-conditioned continuous time Markov chains. Efficient C++ implementation via Rcpp.
